ASSETLENS | HANDOVER

Overview

Handover as Readiness.
Not Just Documentation.

Handover is one of the highest-risk moments in any development project. It is the point where contractor closeout, regulatory approvals, deliverables, asset data, operator preparation, and occupancy readiness all need to align, often under significant schedule pressure. AssetLens Handover Advisory provides structured support across the full handover process, helping align delivery teams and operators on what is truly ready for takeover, what still requires action, and how final asset condition should be recorded before occupancy and operational acceptance. The approach treats handover as a readiness milestone, not a documentation exercise, supporting developers in achieving a clear, controlled, and verifiable transition from construction closeout to operational performance.

Benefits

The Value of Early Operability Review
Early Operability Review

Risks Identified Before They Are Built In

Design stage is the most cost-effective point to identify and resolve operational risk. Early advisory helps prevent issues from being built into delivery, where they become more expensive to correct.

Design Reviewed for Operation, Not Just Delivery

Reviews are conducted through an operator's lens, evaluating how the asset will be managed and maintained, not only whether it meets delivery requirements.

Structured Output, Not Open-Ended Commentary

Each review produces a structured report with prioritised findings, practical recommendations, and a comment matrix to support clear decision-making and resubmission tracking.

Prevents Repeated Issues Across Projects

Findings are captured and structured to reduce repeated design issues across future projects, building practical lessons from real delivery experience.

Lifecycle Continuity

Design Advisory is the first stage in a continuous lifecycle

Support can continue from design through build, handover, and early operations, maintaining continuity of the operator's lens across the project lifecycle.
